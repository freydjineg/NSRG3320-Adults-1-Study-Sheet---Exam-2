😴 Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA) Upper Airway

OSA is recurrent upper airway obstruction during sleep causing repeated apnea and reduced ventilation. The soft tissue of the throat collapses and blocks airflow repeatedly. Patients are typically unaware of the obstruction itself. About 75% of severe OSA is undiagnosed.

Risk Factors
Obesity — #1Male genderAlcohol useSmokingLarge neck circumferenceEnlarged tonsils
Clinical Manifestations — The 3 S's
📌 The 3 S's of OSA

Snoring — loud, chronic, every night
Sleepiness — excessive daytime hypersomnolence
Significant-other report — witnessed pauses/gasping
Also: chronic fatigue, morning headaches, nighttime awakening, unable to fall back asleep

Diagnosis

Polysomnographic study (sleep study) — gold standard. Monitors sleep stages, breathing, O₂ saturation, brain waves, heart rhythm overnight.

Risks of Untreated OSA
Heart attackStrokeDiabetesAlzheimer'sDepressionWeight gainMemory loss
Management — Stepwise
1
MAD (Mandibular Advancement Device) — FIRST LINE. Dental appliance pushes jaw forward to keep airway open.
2
CPAP — ONE constant pressure; prevents airway collapse
3
BiPAP — TWO pressures (higher inhale / lower exhale); easier to breathe against; lower average airway pressure
4
Surgery — Reserved for patients who fail or cannot tolerate device therapy:
Tonsillectomy — effective when enlarged tonsils contribute to airway obstruction
Uvulopalatopharyngoplasty (UPPP) — resection of pharyngeal soft tissue and part of the soft palate/uvula; effective in about 50% for snoring, less for apnea
Nasal septoplasty — corrects gross anatomic nasal septal deformity
Maxillomandibular surgery — advances jaw forward to enlarge the posterior pharyngeal region
⚠️ CPAP vs. BiPAP

CPAP: ONE continuous pressure → prevents airway collapse
BiPAP: TWO pressures (IPAP inhale / EPAP exhale) → easier to breathe against; used when CPAP alone is insufficient or not tolerated

Patient Education
  • Teach how OSA causes their symptoms (fatigue, headaches, brain fog)
  • Correct use of CPAP/BiPAP/MAD — compliance is the challenge
  • Educate about risks of untreated OSA
  • Weight loss, avoid alcohol before bed, side-sleeping all help
🦠 Pneumonia Lower Respiratory Infection

Infection of the pulmonary tissue (bacterial, viral, or fungal). Inflammation produces edema that stiffens the lung, decreases compliance and vital capacity, and causes hypoxemia.

Types by Where Acquired
TypeDefinitionKey Organism
CAPCommunity or <48 hrs of admitS. pneumoniae (most common); viral = RSV, COVID, influenza
HAP48+ hours post-hospitalizationMDROs: MRSA, Pseudomonas, Klebsiella
VAPSubtype of HAP on ventilatorSame MDROs as HAP
Clinical Manifestations
Fever, chillsCough, dyspneaTachypneaRhonchi / wheezingPleuritic chest painPurulent sputumMental status changes
🚨 Older Adults Trap!

Classic signs (fever, cough) may be ABSENT. Confusion or sudden mental status change may be the FIRST and ONLY sign. Never wait for a fever in an elderly patient.

Diagnostics
  • CXR: consolidation, infiltrates, or pleural effusion
  • Sputum culture: collect BEFORE antibiotics when possible
  • Elevated WBC and ESR
Nursing Management
  • Administer oxygen, antibiotics, supportive medications
  • Monitor O₂ sat, labored breathing, cyanosis, cold/clammy skin, sputum
  • Encourage coughing, deep breathing, and IS use
  • Position: semi-Fowler's (30–45°); change position frequently
  • Suction if unable to clear secretions independently
  • Encourage fluids; high-calorie, high-protein diet; balance rest and activity
💡 Why Semi-Fowler's?

Gravity helps the diaphragm descend → lungs expand more fully. Frequent position changes mobilize secretions from different lung fields. IS prevents alveolar collapse (atelectasis) — collapsed alveoli are where pneumonia grows.

Patient Education — Call If:
Chills or feverIncreasing dyspneaHemoptysisIncreased fatiguePneumococcal vaccine recommended
💨 Asthma Chronic Inflammatory Airway

Chronic inflammatory disorder of the airways causing often reversible airflow obstruction. Unlike COPD, obstruction is episodic and can resolve with treatment. Severity based on clinical presentation before treatment is initiated.

4 Pathophysiologic Processes
1
Hyperresponsiveness — airways overreact to triggers/stimuli
2
Bronchiole inflammation — swelling of airway walls
3
Bronchoconstriction — smooth muscle tightens (bronchospasm)
4
Mucus production — thick mucus plugs airways
Clinical Manifestations
Wheezing / cracklesDecreased O₂ satAccessory muscle useTachypneaProlonged exhalationTachycardiaDiaphoresisRestlessness
🚨 Silent Chest = Emergency!

Absent/diminished lung sounds means SO LITTLE air is moving that even wheezing is inaudible. This is MORE dangerous than loud wheezing — it signals impending respiratory failure.

🚨 Status Asthmaticus

Severe, life-threatening episode REFRACTORY to usual treatment. Requires IV corticosteroids, IV magnesium sulfate, possible intubation.

Action Plan — Peak Flow Zones
ZonePeak FlowAction
🟢 Green>80% personal bestAll good — continue daily meds
🟡 Yellow50–79% personal bestWorsening — add rescue med; call provider
🔴 Red<50% personal bestMEDICAL ALERT — rescue med + call 911
Acute Episode Nursing Management
  • Position: High-Fowler's or sitting upright
  • Monitor VS, pulse oximetry, and peak flow
  • Administer oxygen first (correct hypoxemia)
  • Stay with the client to decrease anxiety
  • Administer bronchodilators, corticosteroids, magnesium sulfate as ordered
  • Auscultate lung sounds before, during, and after treatments

💨 COPD — Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease NOT Fully Reversible

Airflow obstruction that is NOT completely reversible. Umbrella term for chronic bronchitis and emphysema, which often coexist. Progressive → pulmonary insufficiency → pulmonary hypertension → cor pulmonale (right heart failure).

ABG: respiratory acidosis + hypoxemia. CXR: congestion and hyperinflation. PFTs: decreased vital capacity.

Nursing Management
  • Monitor VS, pulse ox, ABGs, sputum, weight
  • Administer O₂ — target SaO₂ 88–92%; position in Fowler's leaning forward (tripod)
  • Provide respiratory treatments and chest physiotherapy (CPT)
  • Teach pursed-lip breathing and diaphragmatic breathing
  • Administer bronchodilators, corticosteroids, mucolytics, antibiotics
  • High-calorie, high-protein diet; fluids 2–3 L/day; suction as needed
🚨 Oxygen in COPD — Hypoxic Drive

COPD patients retain CO₂ chronically → become desensitized → breathing trigger shifts to LOW O₂ (hypoxic drive). Too much O₂ removes this drive → respiratory depression. Target SaO₂: 88–92%. Venturi mask gives most precise FiO₂.

💡 Pursed-Lip Breathing Technique

Inhale 2 counts through nose → exhale 4 counts through pursed lips. Creates back-pressure that keeps airways open during exhalation — reduces air trapping. Longer exhale than inhale.

Patient Education (Complete List from Slides)
  • Stop smoking — #1 most important intervention
  • Alternate rest with activity; avoid gas-producing/spicy/extreme temp foods
  • Avoid crowds and people with infections; avoid temperature extremes
  • Avoid fireplaces, pets, feather pillows, powerful odors
  • Receive recommended immunizations (flu, pneumococcal)
  • Recognize signs of infection and hypoxia
  • Use medications and O₂ as prescribed; use pursed-lip and diaphragmatic breathing

🦴 Module 6: Musculoskeletal Disorders & Trauma

11 conditions: OA/arthroplasty, osteoporosis, osteomalacia, Paget's, osteomyelitis, septic arthritis, soft tissue injuries, fractures, compartment syndrome, casts & braces, and amputation.

💡

Labs differentiate bone diseases. The 5 Ps detect compartment syndrome early. Open fractures: COVER — never reduce. Nursing interventions for osteomyelitis and septic arthritis are IDENTICAL per the lecture slides.

🦴 Osteoarthritis (OA) & Arthroplasty Degenerative Joint Disease

OA is wear-and-tear degenerative joint disease — breakdown of articular cartilage leads to damage of the underlying bone. Osteophytes (bone spurs) form in the joint space causing progressive degeneration. OA is MECHANICAL, not systemic.

Risk Factors & Common Joints
Older ageFemale genderObesityLabor-intensive workSports activities

Most affected joints: hips, knees, cervical spine, lumbar spine (weight-bearing)

Clinical Manifestations
  • Pain — hard, bony; aggravated by movement, relieved by rest
  • Morning stiffness — if present, brief (~30 minutes)
  • Crepitus (grinding/crackling)
  • Mild joint effusion; functional impairment
  • NO systemic manifestations — key distinction from RA!
📌 Bony Nodes — Memory Trick

Heberden's nodes = DISTAL (DIP) joints
Bouchard's nodes = PROXIMAL (PIP) joints
HeberDen's = Distal — both have "D"

⚠️ OA vs. RA

OA: NO systemic signs, brief AM stiffness (<30 min), mechanical, X-ray = decreased joint space + osteophytes
RA: SYSTEMIC (fever, fatigue), prolonged stiffness (>1 hr), immune-mediated
Absence of systemic symptoms = OA on NCLEX

Assessment & Diagnostics
  • Crepitus, mild effusion, NO systemic findings
  • If already diagnosed: blood tests and joint fluid exam not necessary
  • X-ray: decreased joint space, osteophyte formation; subchondral bone may be thickened
Management
  • Goals: decrease pain/stiffness; maintain/improve joint motility
  • Exercise, weight loss, OT/PT, orthotic devices/walking aids
  • NSAIDs, steroids; severe OA → total arthroplasty
Arthroplasty — Nursing Interventions
1
Positioning — hip precautions prevent dislocation
2
Incision care; pain management
3
Early ambulation — prevents DVT, PE, and pneumonia
4
Patient education; monitor for post-op complications
🚨 Post-Arthroplasty Complications

DVT/PE (calf pain → dyspnea), Dislocation (sudden severe pain, limb shortening — POSITIONING PREVENTS), Infection (fever, drainage), Hemorrhage/Shock

🦴 Metabolic Bone Diseases Osteoporosis · Osteomalacia · Paget's
Osteoporosis

Bone resorption outpaces formation → thinning and fragility. "Silent thief" — asymptomatic until a fracture occurs.

Female genderSmall frameSteroid useAromatase inhibitorsImmobilityDiabetes

Manifestations: Low BMD on DEXA scan; Dowager's hump; osteoporotic fracture; otherwise asymptomatic

🚨 Labs are ALL NORMAL!

Ca, Phos, Alk Phos all normal. Diagnosed by DEXA scan (T-score) only. X-ray shows radiolucency only if significant demineralization has occurred.

Prevention: High Ca + Vit D diet; weight-bearing exercise 20–30 min/day; calcium supplements; bisphosphonates

Nursing priority: Injury and fall prevention

Osteomalacia

Inadequate bone mineralization from vitamin D deficiency → soft, weakened bones. Adult rickets.

Low sunlightKidney diseaseCrohn's / CeliacMalnutritionLiver disease
🚨 Osteomalacia Labs

LOW Ca ↓ / LOW Phos ↓ / HIGH Alk Phos ↑
Bone biopsy: increased osteoid ("pre-bone")
X-ray: generalized demineralization

Management: Treat underlying cause; supplemental Ca + Vit D; sunlight; if kidney disease → calcitriol (pre-activated Vit D; kidneys can't do this step)

Paget's Disease

Disordered bone remodeling — osteoclasts overactive → osteoblasts overcompensate → large but structurally weak bone. Idiopathic. Affects skull, femur, tibia, pelvic bones, vertebrae.

Often asymptomaticBowing of legsBone tender/warmSpine bent forwardPathologic fractures
🚨 Paget's Labs

HIGH Alk Phos ↑ / NORMAL Ca
X-ray: mosaic pattern; bone overgrowth + demineralization

Management: NSAIDs; Ca + Vit D; bisphosphonates (slow excessive bone turnover)

⚠️ Bisphosphonate Teaching

Morning · empty stomach · full glass of water. Stay UPRIGHT 30–60 min after — prevents esophageal erosion.

🦠 Bone Infections Osteomyelitis · Septic Arthritis
Osteomyelitis — Bone Infection

Infection of bone → inflammation, necrosis, new bone formation. If untreated → bone abscess (chronic osteomyelitis).

Most common organism: S. aureus (increasingly MRSA) — >50% of cases

3 Routes of Infection
1
Hematogenous — blood-borne
2
Contiguous-focus — from surgery or adjacent wound
3
Vascular insufficiency — diabetes, PVD
⚠️ Diabetic Red Flag

Foot ulcer >2 cm = highly suspicious for osteomyelitis. Only ~50% of wound cultures are positive.

Diagnostics

Acute: X-ray, bone scan, MRI; leukocytosis, elevated ESR
Chronic: WBC and ESR may be NORMAL; possible anemia

Management
  • Long-term antibiotic therapy; surgical debridement
  • Prevention: sterile technique; prophylactic antibiotics; prompt drain removal
Septic Arthritis — Joint Infection 🚨

Infection of a joint. Can destroy the joint in hours. Overall mortality rate for single joint infection = 11%.

Older ageDiabetesRASkin infectionAlcoholismIV drug use~50% involve the knee
Clinical Manifestations
Warm, painful, swollen jointDecreased ROMChills, feverLeukocytosis

Key diagnostic test: Aspiration and culture of synovial fluid

🚨 Prompt Treatment is Vital!

Hot, swollen, painful joint with fever needs URGENT aspiration + IV antibiotics. Delays cause permanent joint destruction.

Management
  • Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics; therapeutic aspiration
  • Splinting for comfort; progressive ROM exercises
✅ Same Nursing Priorities as Osteomyelitis! (From slides)

Pain relief → Improve physical mobility → Control infectious process → Promote transitional/home care

24–72 hrs after long-bone/pelvic fracture:
FAT triad: Respiratory distress · Altered mental status · Tiny petechiae on chest/axillae
Petechiae on chest/axillae differentiates from PE

Compartment Syndrome 🚨

Increased pressure within a muscle compartment → impairs blood flow → tissue death. Limb-threatening emergency.

🚨 The 5 Ps — Neurovascular Check

Pain — intense, disproportionate — EARLIEST sign!
Pallor — pale skin, reduced capillary refill
Pulselessness — absent pulse (LATE sign!)
Paresthesia — numbness/tingling
Paralysis — muscle weakness (LATE sign!)
Also: taut, shiny skin with potential blistering

⚠️ Don't Wait for Late Signs!

Act on disproportionate pain not relieved by analgesics — notify provider IMMEDIATELY. Pulselessness and paralysis indicate advanced ischemia.

Definitive treatment: Fasciotomy — surgical incision through fascia to release pressure. Wound left open initially.

Casts & Braces

Assess neurovascular status (5 Ps) BEFORE and AFTER application.

Increasing pain → reportNumbness/tingling → reportPallor/coolness → reportFoul odor/drainage → report
Amputation
  • Priority acute complication: Hemorrhage — keep tourniquet at bedside!
  • Phantom limb pain — real; validate and treat
  • Figure-8 wrapping (BKA) — shapes residual limb for prosthesis
  • Nursing focus: resolving grief and enhancing body image

🔬 Module 7: Endocrine Disorders

Diabetes (Types 1 & 2, emergencies, chronic complications), pituitary (DI, SIADH, acromegaly), adrenal (Addison's, Cushing's), thyroid, parathyroid, and calcium imbalances.

💡

Learn what each hormone DOES normally — "too much" and "too little" write themselves. Most conditions come in HYPER/HYPO pairs that are exact opposites. Ca²⁺ and phosphorus ALWAYS move in opposite directions.

Hormones & Sodium
  • ADH excess → water retained → dilutional hyponatremia (SIADH)
  • ADH deficiency → water lost → hypernatremia (DI)
  • Aldosterone excess → sodium retention → hypernatremia
  • Aldosterone deficiency → sodium loss → hyponatremia (Addison's)
  • Cortisol: high = Na retention + HTN; low = Na loss + hypotension
Calcium, Phosphorus & Hormones
📌 Ca²⁺ & Phos Always Move OPPOSITE

PTH: ↑ Ca (bone breakdown, renal reabsorption, Vit D activation); ↓ Phos (renal excretion)
Vitamin D (calcitriol): ↑ BOTH Ca AND Phos absorption from gut
Calcitonin: ↓ Ca (inhibits osteoclasts) — "Calcitonin TONES DOWN calcium"

🚨 Acute Diabetic Emergencies Hypoglycemia · DKA · HHS
Hypoglycemia (<70 mg/dL)

Causes: too much insulin or sulfonylurea, too little food, excessive exercise

SeverityGlucoseSigns
Mild54–70Shakiness, sweating, hunger, irritability

📌 The 15/15 Rule (Conscious Patient)

If <70 mg/dL and CONSCIOUS → give 15g simple carbs (4 oz juice)
Recheck in 15 minutes → repeat if still low
After 45g with no improvement → IV D50 or IM/SC glucagon
ALTERED LOC: NEVER oral → glucagon or D50 IMMEDIATELY

⚠️ Hypoglycemia Unawareness

Seen with: frequent lows, elderly, beta-blocker use (masks tachycardia warning sign). Dangerous — patient may not feel symptoms until critically low.

📌 Glucagon (From Slides)

Secreted by alpha cells of the pancreas. Raises blood glucose. Given SC, IM, or IV. Blood glucose rises 5–20 min after administration.

🚨 DKA IV Insulin Rules (From Slides)

1. SHORT-ACTING (Regular) insulin ONLY — never long-acting IV
2. Bolus dose may precede continuous infusion
3. Continue IV drip until SC insulin is resumed — stopping early causes rebound hyperglycemia

🧠 Pituitary Disorders DI · SIADH · Acromegaly · Hypophysectomy
Diabetes Insipidus (DI) — Too LITTLE ADH

Kidneys cannot concentrate urine → massive dilute urine output and dehydration.

Large dilute urinePolydipsiaLow specific gravityDehydrationHypernatremiaPostural hypotensionTachycardia

SIADH — Too MUCH ADH

Excess ADH → water retained → dilutional hyponatremia → cerebral edema → seizures.

SIADH: excess ADH → concentrated urine, fluid overload, ↓Na → RESTRICT fluids

📌 NCLEX Q — Slide 52

Most indicative of PRIMARY Addison's? → D: Elevated ACTH levels. Adrenal gland fails → pituitary keeps pushing ACTH up. ACTH also stimulates melanin → bronzing. Secondary Addison's = ACTH is LOW, aldosterone is NORMAL.

Adrenalectomy

Bilateral → lifelong glucocorticoid + mineralocorticoid replacement. Unilateral → usually temporary glucocorticoid only. Adrenal glands are highly vascular — watch for hemorrhage. Monitor for cardiovascular collapse post-op (catecholamine levels drop).

🚨 CATS — Hypocalcemia Signs (ALL Systems)

Convulsions — CNS: also anxiety, irritability, depression, confusion, hallucinations
Arrhythmias — bradycardia, hypotension, diminished pulses; EKG: prolonged ST + QT intervals
Tetany & Tingling — hyperactive DTRs, muscle spasms, Chvostek's, Trousseau's, paresthesias
Stridor & Spasms — laryngospasm (can close airway!); also dyspnea
+ GI: ↑ motility, abdominal cramping, diarrhea, hyperactive bowel sounds
Low calcium = HYPEREXCITABLE nerves and muscles

📌 Chvostek's vs. Trousseau's

Chvostek's: tap the CHEEK (facial nerve) → facial muscle twitching
Trousseau's: inflate BP cuff above systolic → carpal spasm
Both positive = hypocalcemia

⚠️ IV Calcium Safety — Critical Points (Ch. 10)

Dilute in D5W only — NEVER in 0.9% NaCl (saline increases renal calcium loss and defeats the purpose)
Give as SLOW IV bolus or via infusion pump — too rapid → cardiac arrest preceded by bradycardia
VESICANT — extravasation causes severe tissue necrosis/cellulitis; monitor IV site continuously
Digitalis caution — calcium potentiates digoxin toxicity; monitor closely in patients on digitalis-based medications
Ca gluconate vs. Ca chloride: gluconate = 4.5 mEq Ca; chloride = 13.6 mEq Ca — NOT interchangeable; clarify with provider

📌 Hypocalcemia Management — From Slides

1. IV calcium gluconate — slowly, carefully assess site (VESICANT)
2. Vitamin D — increases GI calcium absorption
3. Aluminum hydroxide — reduces phosphorus levels (↓Phos → ↑Ca reciprocally)
4. Seizure precautions — initiated if hypocalcemia is severe
5. Monitor EKG — watch for prolonged QT/torsades
6. Patient education — calcium-rich foods: milk, dairy, leafy greens, canned salmon, fresh oysters

📌 Hypercalcemia Management — From Slides

1. Limit/discontinue calcium intake — hold Ca supplements, Ca-containing antacids, Vit D
2. IV 0.9% NaCl — dilutes serum Ca, promotes urinary Ca excretion
3. Furosemide (NOT thiazides) — loop diuretics excrete calcium; replace any thiazide diuretics (thiazides RETAIN Ca)
4. Inhibit bone resorption: phosphorus, bisphosphonates, calcitonin
5. Monitor for renal stones — assess for flank or abdominal pain
6. Mobilize patient — immobility worsens hypercalcemia; encourage ambulation
7. Dialysis — for severe cases unresponsive to other treatment
